# Navigating Exam Stress

Exams are a common source of significant stress, and it is entirely normal to feel a heavy weight of pressure as they approach. The stakes can feel incredibly high, and the sheer volume of material you are expected to know can seem insurmountable. 

However, exam stress is not a reflection of your intelligence or your capability. It is a reflection of a high-pressure environment. Learning how to navigate this pressure is a skill that takes time to develop.

## The Cycle of Overwhelm

When you are stressed about an exam, it is common to fall into a cycle of avoidance and panic. You might look at your syllabus, feel completely overwhelmed by how much you have to do, and decide to procrastinate. This procrastination leads to guilt and even more stress, which eventually culminates in a frantic cramming session.

This cycle is exhausting and ineffective. To break it, we have to change the way we approach the work itself.

## Breaking It Down

The most effective way to combat the paralysis of overwhelm is to break your tasks down into ridiculously small, manageable pieces. 

Instead of putting "Study for History" on your to-do list, which is vague and intimidating, try "Read three pages of Chapter 4." 

If reading three pages feels too heavy, make it smaller. "Open the textbook and read one paragraph." The goal is to lower the barrier to entry so much that your brain doesn't have the opportunity to resist. Once you start, momentum often carries you forward. But getting started is the hardest part.

## The Myth of Multitasking

In an effort to get more done, you might try to study while watching a video, answering texts, or listening to a podcast. The truth is, the human brain cannot effectively multitask. It can only rapidly switch between tasks, which drains your cognitive energy and significantly reduces your ability to retain information.

When you study, try to do so in short, focused bursts. Set a timer for 25 minutes. During this time, put your phone in another room and focus entirely on the material. When the timer goes off, take a 5-minute break. Step away from your desk, stretch, or get a glass of water. This is known as the Pomodoro Technique, and it helps prevent burnout while maintaining high focus.

## Redefining Success

It is easy to tie your entire sense of self-worth to a grade. If you get an A, you feel valuable. If you fail, you feel worthless. This is a heavy, dangerous burden to carry.

An exam is simply a snapshot of how well you answered a specific set of questions on a specific day. It does not define your character, your future, or your worth as a human being. 

If you don't get the result you were hoping for, it is okay to feel disappointed. But try not to internalize the failure. Ask yourself: "What can I learn from this? How can I adjust my approach next time?" 

## Rest is Productive

One of the biggest mistakes you can make during exam season is sacrificing sleep in order to study more. Sleep is when your brain consolidates memory. If you stay up all night cramming, you are actively preventing your brain from storing the information you are trying to learn.

Treat sleep, nutrition, and downtime as non-negotiable parts of your study routine. Taking a walk, resting your eyes, or simply doing nothing for an hour is not "wasting time." It is necessary maintenance for your brain. You cannot pour from an empty cup, and you cannot think clearly on a sleep-deprived brain. Pace yourself, and remember to breathe.
